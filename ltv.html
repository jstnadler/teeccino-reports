📊 Projected 2-Year LTV — Recent Cohorts
How this works: For each cohort that hasn't reached 24 months yet, we take its actual cumulative LTV at its current age, divide by the % of 2-yr LTV historically captured by that age (the "velocity curve" from complete cohorts), and scale to month 24.
Example: a cohort at 12 months with $90 actual LTV — if complete cohorts captured 73% of their 2-yr value by month 12 — projects to $90 / 0.73 = $123.
Caveats: assumes recent cohorts will retain like historical ones. If product mix or subscriber rate has shifted materially, projections drift. Cohorts younger than 6 months are flagged "very low confidence" — small denominator, high variance.

💰 Contribution Margin LTV vs CAC — Are These Cohorts Actually Profitable?
The real profitability question. Gross LTV (revenue per customer) is just an input; what matters is contribution margin per customer compared to cost to acquire them.

Contribution Margin LTV = Gross LTV − COGS − Shipping − Payment Processing.
  • COGS: SUM(line items × cogs_per_unit) per order — ~99% revenue coverage from the COGS table
  • Shipping: actual ShipStation cost per order when matched (2024+); $7.50 fallback for pre-2024 orders
  • Processing: 2.9% × revenue + $0.30/txn (Shopify Payments standard)

CAC = (Meta + Google ad spend in cohort acquisition month) ÷ (# new DTC customers acquired in that month). Treats all paid spend as acquisition cost — slightly overstates CAC since some spend goes to retargeting.

Profitable cohort: CM-LTV / CAC ≥ 1.0x means the cohort's 2-year contribution margin covers what we paid to acquire them. Industry rule of thumb: ≥3x is healthy.
What's NOT modeled: returns/refunds, organic costs (Klaviyo/Attentive subscriptions), warehousing, support, taxes. So actual cohort profit is somewhat lower than CM-LTV.
